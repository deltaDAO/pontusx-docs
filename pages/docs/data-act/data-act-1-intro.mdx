# The EU Data Act 

## Executive Summary 
The Data Act: Why your data strategy is now a top priority.
The European digital economy is on the cusp of a historic turning point. With the entry into force of the EU Data Act (Regulation (EU) 2023/2854), the legislator is completing the transition from a defensive data protection culture to a proactive data utilisation policy. The aim is to mobilise industrial data that has hitherto been locked away in proprietary silos belonging to original equipment manufacturers (OEMs) and hyperscalers. For European small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), this opens significant opportunities to regain data sovereignty and tap into new value chains, but at the same time entails considerable technical and legal complexity. 

## Introduction: Europe’s new data order 

### The regulatory change

For a long time, ownership of data was synonymous with exclusive control over value creation. Manufacturers of connected machines and vehicles were able to dominate the aftermarket for maintenance and services through proprietary interfaces and closed ecosystems. The EU Data Act breaks this logic by legally stipulating that the user of a device – and not just the manufacturer – may determine how the generated data is used. This is not merely a compliance issue, but a direct intervention in the market structure. 

The relevance for C-level decision-makers in SMEs stems from the dual nature of the law: on the one hand, it creates new avenues for accessing data that is necessary for optimising their own processes (e.g. predictive maintenance without being tied to a specific manufacturer). On the other hand, it compels companies that manufacture smart products themselves to open up their data architectures. Those who are unprepared here risk not only fines but also the loss of strategic competitive advantages. 

### The role of data spaces as infrastructure 
In this context, data spaces are evolving from abstract reference architectures into critical infrastructure. The Data Act not only mandates access to data, but also explicitly requires interoperability in Chapter VIII. A simple data export does not meet the requirements for modern, automated value creation. Pontus-X provides the technological framework to technically enforce the legal requirements for fairness, security and interoperability (“Code is Law”). Pontus-X acts as a transaction layer that standardises data exchange and makes it monetisable.
