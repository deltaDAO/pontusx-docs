# The EU Data Act 

## Detailed Analysis
The Data Act is at the heart of the European data strategy. Its provisions are far-reaching and affect virtually every sector of the manufacturing industry. A precise analysis of the articles reveals the depth of the impact on existing B2B relationships. 

### The right of access and use (Chapter II) 

The fundamental principle of the Data Act is set out in Articles 3 to 5. It states that products must be designed in such a way that data is easily accessible by default (‘Access by Design’). 

- **Art. 3: Access "by Design".** Manufacturers must design IoT devices in such a way that users have direct access to the data generated. 
- **Art. 4: Users’ rights.** Users may require the data controller to provide the data immediately and free of charge. 
- **Art. 5: Disclosure to third parties.** Users may instruct that data be transferred directly to third parties (e.g. service providers). 
- **Art. 8: FRAND principles.** Data must be provided on fair, reasonable and non-discriminatory terms. 

### The protection of trade secrets in the context of transparency 
One of the most critical issues for European businesses is the protection of intellectual property. If raw data from a machine must be disclosed, competitors could use reverse engineering to draw conclusions about patented control algorithms or manufacturing processes. 

The Data Act acknowledges this risk in Article 4(8) and Article 8(6). It establishes a ‘protect-then-share’ approach. Data controllers may refuse access if they can demonstrate that, despite technical safeguards, the disclosure of trade secrets would result in ‘serious economic harm’. However, this is not a blanket exemption. 

The data owner must: 

- Attempt to agree on technical and organisational measures (TOMs) with the user to preserve confidentiality. 
- In the event of a refusal, report this to the national supervisory authority and provide reasons. 

This creates a significant bureaucratic risk. The solution lies in technologies that enable access without compromising confidentiality – a key value proposition of  Pontus-X.  

### Interoperability as a legal requirement (Chapter VIII) 

Chapter VIII (Articles 33–36) transforms technical standards into legal obligations. Operators of data spaces must ensure that data, data-sharing mechanisms and services are interoperable. 

This includes: 

- **Data set contents**: Description of data structures and vocabularies in machine-readable form. 
- **Access interfaces**: Disclosure of APIs for automated data transfer. 
- **Smart contracts**: Article 36 sets out specific requirements for smart contracts used for data exchange, including robustness, access control and termination. 

This regulation effectively forces companies to move away from proprietary siloed solutions and adopt open standards.
